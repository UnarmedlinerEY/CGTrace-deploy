Calculation logic overview — three regimes
A — Deemed sale & deferred gain (post-1985 asset, sold after 1 Jul 2027)
In plain English: the Bill treats every asset you hold at 30 June 2027 as if you sold it just before 1 July 2027 and bought it back at its value on that date. You don't pay anything at that point — the notional gain is deferred and only comes to account when you actually sell. At that later sale, the pre-2027 part is taxed under the old rules (50% discount) and the post-2027 part under the new rules (CPI indexation + 30% minimum). The previous "transitional split" framing in v4 was structurally right; the Bill's own language is deemed sale / deferred notional gain.
Applies when: acquired before 1 Jul 2027 · sold after 1 Jul 2027 · held ≥12 months · not a company · not a new build · not a pre-CGT asset · not foreign-resident disqualified.

Step 1 — Establish the reacquisition value at 1 July 2027 — MV or apportioning method (ss.112-155(2), 112-175(3), 112-185; instrument not yet made)
The Bill sets reacquisition at market value, or at an amount worked out under an apportioning method that may be prescribed by the Minister by legislative instrument — the instrument has not been made, so both formulas below remain proxies. Critically, the Bill lets you defer the MV-vs-method choice until you lodge the return for the year of actual realisation — keep both options open and secure valuation evidence near 1 July 2027. Select between proxy formulas in the Inputs tab under "Formula type".
METHOD A — Linear time-apportionment (industry proxy — most widely used) Val(1 Jul 2027) = Cost base + (Sale price − Cost base) × (Pre-days ÷ Total days) Assumes: gain accrues at a CONSTANT dollar amount per day. Risk: overstates 2027 value for assets with late-period surges (e.g. property 2020–22 boom). understates 2027 value for assets with early-period surges. Status: ⚠ ASSUMED — ATO formula not released. METHOD B — Compound growth rate (implied by Bill: "average rate of growth") CAGR = (Sale price ÷ Cost base)^(1 ÷ Total years) − 1 Val(1 Jul 2027) = Cost base × (1 + CAGR)^Pre-years Assumes: gain compounds at a CONSTANT ANNUAL RATE throughout holding period. Difference: in rising markets, compound growth front-loads value to earlier years, producing a HIGHER 2027 value than linear → more gain in pre-2027 (50% disc) bucket. Status: ⚠ ASSUMED — inferred from Bill wording only. WORKED EXAMPLE (purchase $500k Jul 2020, sell $900k Jul 2029): Linear: Val(2027) = $500k + $400k × (2,557÷3,287) = $811,200 Compound: CAGR = (900k/500k)^(1/9.0)−1 = 6.75%/yr Val(2027) = $500k × (1.0675)^7.0 = $779,400 ← lower in this example Difference: $31,800 — pushes $31,800 more gain into POST-2027 bucket under compound method. KEY INSIGHT: Which method is more favourable depends on whether the asset outperformed its average CAGR before or after 2027. Use market valuation where possible — it eliminates both formula risks entirely.
Step 2 — Pre-2027 tax on the deferred gain (existing law)
Deferred (pre-2027) gross gain = Val(1 Jul 2027) − pre-2027 cost base. If the asset was under water at the transition, this is a deferred capital LOSS instead — it crystallises at realisation and joins Step 1 current-year losses.
Losses then apply per the method statement order (Steps 1–2: deferred non-res → deferred res → non-res → res).
50% CGT discount at Step 5 (≥12 months; acquisition date preserved by ss.112-160(3)(c),(5) / 112-170(3)(c),(5) — the deemed event does not restart the clock).
Pre gain = $811,200 − $500,000 = $311,200 After losses: $311,200 − $0 = $311,200 Discounted: $311,200 × 50% = $155,600 Pre-2027 tax (@ 47%): $155,600 × 47% = $73,132
Step 3 — Post-2027 tax — expense-by-expense quarterly indexation (v6 expert)
In plain English: every dollar you spend on the asset from 1 July 2027 gets its own inflation uplift, measured from the calendar quarter you spent it to the quarter you sell. The 1 July 2027 transition value counts as your first "expense". Ownership costs (Element 3) never get the uplift. You need 12 months of ownership for any indexation at all.
The deemed reacquisition (s.112-155(2)) makes V(1 Jul 2027) the first cost base element, indexed from Q3-2027. Each later expense in elements 1, 2, 4 and 5 carries its own factor: the ABS All Groups CPI index number for the quarter of the CGT event divided by the index number for the quarter the expense was incurred. Element 3 is excluded. Future index numbers don't exist yet, so this tool projects them from your CPI assumption, compounding quarterly (⚠ ASSUMED rate, CONFIRMED mechanics).
For each expense e in elements 1, 2, 4, 5 incurred on/after 1 Jul 2027: factor(e) = index(quarter of CGT event) ÷ index(quarter e incurred) — projected here as (1 + CPI)^(Δquarters ÷ 4) Indexed CB = V(1 Jul 2027) × factor(Q3-2027) + Σ indexed post-2027 expenses (el. 1/2/4/5) + Σ Element 3 expenses (unindexed) Requires ≥12 months holding (acquisition date preserved — ss.112-160(3)(c),(5)). EXAMPLE (this tool's default scenario, ledger = single $500k acquisition): V(2027) = $811,200 (Step 1, linear proxy) · sale 1 Jul 2029 · CPI 2.8% Indexed CB = $811,200 × (1.028)^(8 qtrs ÷ 4) = $857,300 A $30,000 element-4 renovation in Sep 2028 would add $30,000 × (1.028)^(3 qtrs ÷ 4) = $30,630 to the indexed CB. Post-2027 real gain = $900,000 − $857,300 = $42,700 SIMPLE proxy: tax = MAX($42,700 × 47%, $42,700 × 30%) = $20,069 FULL gap method (Bill mechanics — toggle in Inputs): The post-2027 real gain is the "minimum tax capital gain". additional tax = MAX(0, 30% × $42,700 − tax actually attributable to it at resident marginal rates, given other income & deductions) Deductions reduce ordinary tax but CANNOT pull the rate on this gain below 30% — effective rates can exceed 47% in high-deduction years (PwC, 1 Jun 2026).
Step 4 — Total
Total tax = $73,132 + $20,069 = $93,201 After-tax proceeds = $900,000 − $93,201 = $806,799 Effective rate on total gain = $93,201 ÷ $400,000 = 23.3% Compare — old law (full 50% disc on whole gain): Tax = ($400k × 50%) × 47% = $94,000 ← similar in this example New law cost = $93,201 ← slightly cheaper here (CPI erodes post-2027 gain)
B — Pre-CGT asset (acquired before 20 Sep 1985, sold after 1 Jul 2027)
What changed in the Bill (28 May 2026): the deemed sale/reacquisition at 1 July 2027 applies to all pre-CGT assets and all entities — including companies (the previous version of this tool wrongly excluded companies). Reacquisition is at MV by default, or under the apportioning method — the earlier statement that "there is no formula alternative for pre-CGT assets" was wrong and is corrected. The instrument is not yet made, so only the MV pathway is computable here.
Why there is still no pre-2027 taxable component for pre-CGT assets:
The deferred-gain split needs a taxable pre-2027 gain to apply the 50% discount to. Pre-CGT assets have none — the entire pre-2027 gain is permanently exempt by statute. The deemed reacquisition simply installs a fresh cost base at 1 July 2027; everything above it is fully exposed to the new regime.

Step 1 — Pre-2027: zero tax (permanent exemption)
All appreciation from acquisition to 1 Jul 2027 is permanently CGT-exempt. This is not a discount — it simply does not exist as a taxable amount. Sell before 1 Jul 2027 = zero tax on the entire gain.
Step 2 — Establish deemed cost base at 1 July 2027
Market value at 1 Jul 2027 becomes the entire cost base — the asset is deemed reacquired on that date. This is now CONFIRMED in the Bill (deemed sale just before 1 Jul 2027, reacquisition at MV or apportioning method), applying to all entities including companies. A formal valuation prepared close to 1 July 2027 remains essential evidence.
Deemed cost base = MV at 1 Jul 2027 (e.g. $820,000 for a property worth $900k at sale) Alternative: the apportioning method (Ministerial legislative instrument) is ALSO available for pre-CGT assets under the Bill — but the instrument is not yet made, so this tool computes the MV pathway only. ⚠ PENDING.
Step 3 — Post-2027 tax: the indexation trap
Indexation only runs FORWARD from 1 Jul 2027. Unlike a post-1985 transitional asset where CPI compounds over decades of holding history, a pre-CGT asset gets zero years of historic CPI benefit. Every dollar of real growth above the 2027 MV is fully exposed to the 30% minimum tax.
Indexed CB = MV(1 Jul 2027) × (1 + CPI%)^years_held_post_2027 Example: MV at 1 Jul 2027 = $820,000 · Sale price = $900,000 · Sale date = 1 Jul 2029 · CPI 2.8% Indexed CB = $820,000 × (1.028)^2 = $866,700 (approx) Post-2027 real gain = $900,000 − $866,700 = $33,300 Post-2027 tax = MAX($33,300 × 47%, $33,300 × 30%) = $15,651 Compare if SOLD BEFORE 1 JUL 2027 at $900k: tax = $0 (full exemption) TAX SAVING FROM SELLING EARLY = $15,651 in this example
Critical risk: no indexation history = maximum new-regime exposure
A post-1985 transitional asset held for 30 years benefits from 30 years of CPI compounding on the cost base — potentially making the new regime cheaper than old law at high CPI. A pre-CGT asset gets NONE of this benefit. The indexed cost base only starts growing at 2027 market value. For high-value pre-CGT assets held for decades, the cost of waiting past 1 Jul 2027 can be very substantial.
C — Loss ordering — now PRESCRIBED by the Bill (28 May 2026)
In plain English: under current law you can choose which gains to put your losses against — and the smart move is usually to shelter the gains taxed hardest. The Bill removes that choice. Capital losses, carry-forward losses and quarantined rental amounts must be applied against deferred (pre-2027) discount gains first, and only the remainder flows to indexed (post-2027) gains. This locks in what was previously this tool's "conservative assumption" — and it is generally the less favourable order for the taxpayer.
PRESCRIBED ORDER (CONFIRMED — new s.102-5 method statement): Step 1 Current-year losses (incl. deferred losses crystallised at the deemed event) across categories in fixed order: deferred non-res → deferred res → non-res → res Step 2 Carry-forward net capital losses, same order Step 3 Quarantined amounts → deferred RESIDENTIAL gains Step 4 Remaining quarantined amounts → residential gains (quarantined amounts NEVER reach non-residential gains) Value of $10,000 of losses at 47% MTR: Against a discount gain (forced first): $10,000 × 50% × 47% = $2,350 saved Against an indexed gain (only with remainder): $10,000 × 47% = $4,700 → The prescribed order costs up to $2,350 per $10,000 of losses versus the choice available under current law. PLANNING CONSEQUENCE: losses realised BEFORE 1 Jul 2027 can still be applied under current-law flexibility; losses carried into the new regime are forced into the prescribed order.
D — Deemed sale, reacquisition and the deferred notional gain
In plain English: nothing is payable on 1 July 2027. The deemed sale fixes a value, the asset is deemed reacquired at that value, and the notional pre-2027 gain rides along untaxed until you actually sell. At that point two components come to account together: the deferred gain (old rules, 50% discount) and the post-2027 gain (indexed cost base, 30% minimum).
At 1 Jul 2027 (no cash event): Deemed disposal just before 1 Jul 2027 at V₂₀₂₇ (MV or apportioning method) Deferred notional gain = V₂₀₂₇ − original cost base [held, not taxed] Deemed reacquisition at V₂₀₂₇ → new cost base for indexation At actual realisation (e.g. 2030): Component 1 — deferred gain: existing law incl. 50% discount (post loss-ordering) Component 2 — post-2027 gain: proceeds − V₂₀₂₇ indexed by CPI from 1 Jul 2027 The MV-vs-method choice for V₂₀₂₇ is made when lodging THIS year's return. 1985–1999 assets: the old frozen-indexation choice is REMOVED — only the 50% discount applies to the deferred pre-2027 component.
E — CGT event K6 at 1 July 2027 (pre-CGT shares & unlisted trust units)
In plain English: K6 is the long-standing anti-avoidance rule that stops you hiding post-1985 property inside a pre-1985 company or trust. If you hold pre-CGT shares in a private company (or units in an unlisted trust) and at least 75% of its net value comes from post-CGT property, selling the shares triggers a gain on that underlying property. The Bill effectively forces a K6 calculation at 1 July 2027: the latent gain is measured and crystallised then, but the tax is deferred until you actually dispose of the interest.
Trigger: pre-CGT shares in private company / units in unlisted trust AND post-CGT property ≥ 75% of net value (s.104-230 ITAA 1997) Deemed-event gains are otherwise disregarded for pre-CGT assets — K6 is the single exception, and even then the gain is deferred. At 1 Jul 2027: latent K6 gain measured (valuation exercise) and crystallised At realisation: deferred K6 gain brought to account alongside the post-2027 gain on the interest itself Tool treatment: user enters the latent K6 gain in Inputs (pre-CGT row). ⚠ ASSUMED: existing-law treatment (50% discount if held >12m) applies to the deferred K6 amount at realisation — Bill detail on its character is pending.
F — New seven-step method statement, four categories & trustee reporting
In plain English: the Bill rewrites how your net capital gain is assembled (s.102-5). Gains are first sorted into four categories — deferred residential, deferred non-residential, residential and non-residential — then losses are applied in the prescribed order, discounts/indexation are applied per category, and the minimum tax is tested against the post-2027 ("minimum tax") gains. Trustees must report these categories to beneficiaries, with administrative penalties for failure.
Seven-step method statement (new s.102-5 — as introduced): 1. Separate gains into FOUR categories — deferred non-residential, deferred residential, non-residential, residential — then apply CURRENT-YEAR capital losses in that category order (including any 30 Jun 2027 deferred losses crystallised in the realisation year) 2. Apply CARRY-FORWARD net capital losses, same order 3. Apply quarantined amounts → deferred residential gains 4. Apply remaining quarantined amounts → residential gains 5. Apply the 50% discount to qualifying deferred gains (≥12m, incl. 1985–99 assets that lose the s.110-36 frozen-indexation choice) and to new-dwelling (50%) / affordable-housing (60%, s.115-125) gains 6. Apply the small business CGT concessions (retained; tested at the realisation event for BOTH components — not at 30 Jun 2027) 7. Total the remainder = NET CAPITAL GAIN Minimum tax (new Division 119 ITAA 1997 — s.119-5 the "minimum tax capital gain", s.119-10 the gap method, s.119-15 the income-support exception; rate set by s.12AA Income Tax Rates Act 1986): applies to the "minimum tax capital gain" — residential + non-residential gains AFTER Step 6 (deferred gains EXCLUDED, even when realised post-2027); extra tax = the "minimum tax gap amount". Applies to individuals (incl. via s.115-215 trust attribution) resident at any time in the income year; prescribed income-support recipients exempt. Trusts (subdiv 115-C, extended): 50% discount on the deferred gain requires a non-foreign/temporary-resident beneficiary specifically entitled to it; indexation is "backed out" for ineligible beneficiaries (e.g. corporate); streaming preserved even where indexation produces no trust net gain. Trustee beneficiary statements per a Commissioner's legislative instrument; administrative penalties under the TAA 1953. See the Steps 1–7 waterfall card in the Tax comparison tab.

Formula deep-dive — proxy validity & ATO precedent
How the linear time-apportionment formula works
The formula estimates what an asset was worth at a specific point (1 July 2027) by assuming its value grew in a straight line from purchase to sale. It takes the total nominal gain and allocates it proportionally based on how many days of the total holding period fell before versus after the transition date.
Val(1 Jul 2027) = Cost base + Total gain × (Pre-2027 days ÷ Total days) Example: Purchased: 1 Jul 2018 Cost base: $500,000 Sale date: 1 Jul 2030 Sale price: $1,100,000 Total gain: $600,000 Total days: 4,383 Pre-2027 days (1 Jul 2018 → 1 Jul 2027): 3,287 Val(1 Jul 2027) = $500,000 + $600,000 × (3,287 ÷ 4,383) = $500,000 + $600,000 × 74.99% = $500,000 + $449,940 = $949,940
Where this formula is already accepted by the ATO
Straight-line time-apportionment is not new to Australian tax law. The ATO has accepted it in several analogous CGT contexts, giving weight to its use as an interim proxy here:
1. Partial main residence exemption — s118-185 ITAA 1997
Where a property was used as a main residence for only part of the ownership period, the CGT-exempt portion is calculated by time-apportionment: exempt days ÷ total days. The ATO's own guidance (CGT record-keeping tool) uses this exact linear approach. ATO QC 66494
2. Mixed-use rental property — income vs private use apportionment
For holiday homes and mixed-use properties, the ATO accepts time-based apportionment to split deductions and capital gains between income-producing and private periods. Linear allocation across days is the standard method. ATO guidance: Rental properties 2025
3. Pre-1999 CGT indexation (prior regime)
Before September 1999, the ATO required indexed cost bases using CPI quarterly indices compounded over the holding period — a more complex but structurally similar time-based approach. The ATO's acceptance of CPI-compounding as a proxy for real asset growth is well-established. The 2027 formula represents a return to that principle.
4. Tax consolidation — allocable cost amount apportionment
In corporate group consolidations, the ATO uses time-weighted and proportional formulas to allocate asset values at joining dates — conceptually identical to splitting a gain at a nominated date using a formula rather than an actual valuation.
Key limitation — why market valuation is preferred when available
Linear apportionment assumes constant growth rate. Real assets rarely grow linearly — property often surges in specific years (e.g. 2020–2022 boom), shares may compound faster in later years. If growth was concentrated in the pre-2027 period, the formula may underestimate the 2027 value, pushing more of the gain into the post-2027 taxable bucket. A formal market valuation that correctly captures where growth actually occurred produces a more accurate (and often more favourable) outcome.
Glossary — plain English guide for non-tax professionals
This glossary explains every key term used in this tool in plain language. No tax background needed.
Core CGT concepts
Capital Gains Tax (CGT) FOUNDATION
A tax you pay when you sell something for more than you paid for it. It's not a separate tax — it gets added to your regular income for the year and taxed at your income tax rate. You only pay it when you sell, not while you hold the asset.
Example: You buy shares for $10,000 and sell them for $16,000. The $6,000 profit is a "capital gain" and CGT applies to it.
Capital gain
The profit you make when you sell an asset for more than it cost you. Calculated as: Sale price minus your cost base (what you paid, plus any buying costs like stamp duty or brokerage).
Capital loss
When you sell something for less than it cost you. Losses can't reduce your regular income — they can only be used to reduce capital gains in the same year or future years. They carry forward indefinitely until you have a gain to offset them against.
Example: You lost $5,000 on crypto last year. This year you make a $20,000 gain on shares. You can subtract the $5,000 loss, leaving only $15,000 as your taxable gain.
Cost base
What you officially "paid" for an asset in tax terms. It's not just the purchase price — it also includes buying costs (stamp duty, legal fees, brokerage), improvement costs (renovations for property), and selling costs. A higher cost base means a smaller taxable gain.
50% CGT discount OLD LAW
The current rule (until 30 June 2027): if you've owned an asset for more than 12 months, you only pay tax on half of your gain. The other half is completely ignored. This is the main tax benefit of long-term investing and is being phased out by the 2026 Budget.
Example: $100,000 gain, held 3 years. Taxable amount = $50,000. At 47% tax rate, you pay $23,500 instead of $47,000.
Marginal tax rate (MTR)
The tax rate that applies to the last dollar of your income. In Australia, the more you earn, the higher rate you pay on the top portion. Capital gains get added to your income and taxed at this top rate. The current maximum is 47% (including Medicare levy).
New 2026 Budget terms
CPI indexation NEW LAW from 1 Jul 2027
The replacement for the 50% discount. Instead of halving your gain, you adjust your cost base upward for inflation (CPI = Consumer Price Index, which measures how prices rise over time). This means you only pay tax on your "real" gain above inflation — not on the part that just kept up with rising prices. If inflation was high, this could be better than the 50% discount. If inflation was low, it's usually worse.
Example: Paid $500,000 in 2020. CPI grew 2.8%/year for 8 years. Inflation-adjusted cost base = ~$634,000. If you sell for $900,000, you only pay tax on $266,000 (the real gain), not the full $400,000.
30% minimum tax NEW LAW from 1 Jul 2027
A new floor tax rate on capital gains under the new regime. Even if your normal income tax rate on the indexed gain would be less than 30%, you still pay at least 30%. This mainly affects people with low incomes who happen to sell an asset — they can no longer enjoy a very low CGT rate.
Example: Retired investor with $20,000 income sells shares with $50,000 indexed gain. Normal rate might be 19%, but they still pay 30% × $50,000 = $15,000 minimum.
Transitional split KEY 2027 MECHANIC
What happens to assets you bought before 1 July 2027 but sell after that date. Your total gain gets split at 1 July 2027: the portion earned before that date gets the old 50% discount, and the portion earned after that date gets the new indexation + 30% min rule. You need a valuation (or formula) to establish what the asset was worth on 1 July 2027.
Val at 1 July 2027 (transition value)
The estimated or actual market value of your asset exactly on 1 July 2027. This is the key number for the transitional split — it separates your pre-2027 gain (old rules) from your post-2027 gain (new rules). You can use a formal professional valuation or the ATO's formula (not yet published).
Apportioning method (legislative instrument)
A mathematical shortcut to estimate what your asset was worth on 1 July 2027, without needing a formal valuation. Under the Bill it is prescribed by the Minister via legislative instrument — which hasn't been made yet. This tool offers two proxies (straight-line and compound growth) until the instrument is released. Helpfully, you don't have to choose between market value and the method until you lodge the tax return for the year you actually sell.
Bill mechanics — passed both Houses 25 Jun 2026 v5/v6
Cost base elements (1–5) & the expense ledger
Your cost base is built from five "elements" (s.110-25): what you paid (1), buying/selling costs like stamp duty and brokerage (2), ownership costs like rates and insurance (3), capital improvements (4), and title costs (5). Under the Bill, each expense in elements 1, 2, 4 and 5 spent from 1 July 2027 gets its own inflation uplift from the quarter you spent it — Element 3 never does. That's why this tool uses a dated, element-tagged ledger.
Index number & quarterly factor
The "index number" is the ABS All Groups Consumer Price Index figure for a calendar quarter. Each expense's uplift factor is the index number for the quarter you sell divided by the index number for the quarter you spent the money. Future quarters aren't published yet, so the tool projects them from your CPI assumption.
Deferred capital loss
If your asset was worth less at 1 July 2027 than its cost base, the deemed sale locks in a loss instead of a gain. Like a deferred gain, it waits until you actually sell — then it counts as a current-year capital loss at Step 1 of the method statement.
Minimum tax gap amount
The extra tax that tops you up to 30% on your minimum tax capital gain. If your marginal rate already taxes that gain at 30% or more, the gap amount is zero.
Specific entitlement (trusts)
A beneficiary is "specifically entitled" to a capital gain when trust paperwork clearly earmarks that gain for them. Under the Bill it matters more than ever: the 50% discount on a trust's deferred gain needs a non-foreign beneficiary specifically entitled to it, and indexation gets backed out if the entitled beneficiary (e.g. a company) isn't eligible.
Treasury Laws Amendment (Tax Reform No. 1) Bill 2026
The actual legislation implementing the 2026–27 Budget CGT changes, introduced to Parliament on 28 May 2026 together with the Income Tax Rates Amendment (Tax Reform No. 1) Bill 2026. The Senate Economics Legislation Committee reported on 19 June 2026 recommending passage (Coalition dissent); the Senate passed the bill with amendments and the House agreed on 25 June 2026. Awaiting Royal Assent — a formality at this stage. Commencement remains 1 July 2027; the core CGT mechanics modelled in this tool are unchanged from the bill as introduced.
Deemed sale & reacquisition
The Bill's core mechanic: every asset you hold at 30 June 2027 is treated as if you sold it just before 1 July 2027 and immediately bought it back at its value on that date. No tax is payable at that moment — it simply fixes the dividing line between old-rules gains and new-rules gains.
Deferred notional gain
The paper profit created by the deemed sale (value at 1 July 2027 minus your original cost base). It sits untaxed until you actually sell, at which point it's taxed under the old rules — including the 50% discount — alongside the post-2027 gain taxed under the new rules.
Minimum tax capital gain / gap method
The post-1 July 2027 portion of your gain is the "minimum tax capital gain". The 30% minimum is an extra tax equal to the gap between 30% of that gain and the tax you'd otherwise pay on it at your marginal rates. Deductions can reduce tax on your salary, but they can't pull the rate on this gain below 30% — which can push effective rates above 47% in some years.
CGT event K6
An anti-avoidance rule (s.104-230) for pre-1985 shares in private companies or units in unlisted trusts, where 75% or more of the entity's net value comes from property acquired after 1985. The Bill effectively requires a K6 calculation at 1 July 2027 — measuring any hidden gain in the underlying post-1985 property — with the tax deferred until you actually sell your shares or units.
Four capital-gain categories
The Bill sorts every capital gain into one of four buckets: deferred residential, deferred non-residential, residential and non-residential. "Deferred" = the pre-2027 portion crystallised by the deemed sale. The buckets matter because losses follow a prescribed order across them and quarantined rental amounts can only offset the residential buckets.
Quarantined rental amounts
Rental losses that can no longer be deducted against your salary under the negative-gearing reform. They carry forward and can be used against future rental income or residential capital gains — but never against shares, crypto or other non-residential gains, and they can't be added to your cost base.
Trustee beneficiary statement
A new reporting obligation: trustees must tell each beneficiary how much of their distribution falls into each of the four capital-gain categories, so the beneficiary can work out their own discount, indexation and minimum-tax position. Administrative penalties apply for getting it wrong. The exact format hasn't been prescribed yet.
1985–1999 frozen indexation (removed)
Assets bought between 20 September 1985 and 21 September 1999 currently have a choice between the 50% discount and cost-base indexation frozen at September 1999. The Bill removes that choice — after 1 July 2027 only the 50% discount applies to the deferred pre-2027 portion of these assets.
Small business rollover relief (3 years)
A transitional concession: small business restructures get rollover relief for three years from 1 July 2027, softening the regime change for business asset reorganisations.
Asset types
Pre-CGT asset
An asset bought before 20 September 1985 — the date CGT was introduced in Australia. These assets have been completely exempt from CGT for 42 years. From 1 July 2027, only the gains earned after that date will be taxed. All growth before 1 July 2027 remains permanently exempt.
Main residence / PPOR (Principal Place of Residence)
Your primary home — the one you actually live in. The sale of your main residence is completely exempt from CGT in Australia. This exemption is unchanged by the 2026 Budget. The "6-year rule" also lets you keep the exemption for up to 6 years even after you move out, as long as you're renting the property.
New build property
A newly constructed residential property that genuinely adds to Australia's housing supply. Under the 2026 Budget, investors in new builds get a special choice at the time of sale: they can use either the old 50% CGT discount OR the new indexation method — whichever gives a better outcome. This is only available to the first purchaser of a new build.
Established property
An existing residential property that was already built and lived in before you bought it. These don't get the new-build regime choice — the transitional split rules apply instead after 1 July 2027.
Investor structures
Discretionary trust
A legal structure where a trustee has flexibility to distribute income and gains to family members or other beneficiaries. Often used for tax planning. From 1 July 2028, trusts will face a new 30% minimum tax on all income — not just capital gains. This is separate from the CGT changes.
SMSF (Self-Managed Super Fund)
A private superannuation fund you control yourself. SMSFs currently get a 1/3 CGT discount on assets held over 12 months (instead of the individual 50% discount). This is expected to remain unchanged by the 2026 Budget, but hasn't been legislated yet.
Tool-specific terms
Break-even price
The minimum sale price you'd need to accept if selling before 1 July 2027 to end up with the same amount of money after tax as selling at a higher price after 2027 (under the new regime). If the break-even price is lower than your current market value, selling early makes financial sense.
Effective tax rate (on gain)
Your actual tax as a percentage of your total gain — not your marginal rate. Because of the 50% discount or indexation, your effective rate is almost always lower than your marginal rate. Use this to compare regimes fairly, especially when gains are different sizes.
Example: $100,000 gain, 50% discount, 47% MTR → tax = $23,500 → effective rate = 23.5% (not 47%).
Negative gearing
When your investment property costs more to hold (loan interest, rates, maintenance) than the rent you receive. The shortfall can currently be deducted against all your other income, reducing your tax bill. From 1 July 2027, this offset is restricted for established properties bought after 7:30pm on 12 May 2026 — those losses can only be used against future rental income from the same property.
